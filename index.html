Child Support Journey Lab · System Log Report

A system built to recover money, serving families who need a service.

The U.S. child support program runs on enforcement machinery designed in 1975 to reimburse the government for welfare. In 2026, most of the families it touches need something different: a process that is legible, dignified, and humane. This report maps the whole system — from the macro structure down to what a single parent feels at the counter.

Level 01zoom: 100,000 ft · the system

Macro — the system

Who runs it, who pays for it, what it was built to do, and the gap between that mission and the families standing in front of it today.

A federal–state–tribal machine

Child support is a single federal program delivered in dozens of different ways. The federal Office of Child Support Services (OCSS, within HHS / Administration for Children and Families) sets the rules, funds the bulk of the cost, audits performance, and operates the national locate tools — but it does not serve a single family directly. Delivery happens locally. All 50 states plus the District of Columbia, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands operate programs, generally at the county level, and roughly 60 tribal nations run their own programs under tribal law and tribal courts.

Two structural facts shape everything downstream. First, Washington reimburses states at a 66% open-ended federal match and layers performance incentives on top under §458 of the Social Security Act — and those incentives reward five throughput metrics (paternity established, orders established, current collections, arrears collections, cost-effectiveness), not whether anyone felt treated fairly. Second, because actual delivery is local, "it depends on the state" is the honest answer to almost every question a family asks — which is itself a structural barrier, not a footnote.

An identity it has never resolved

The program was created in 1975 as a cost-recovery mechanism — a way to repay the government for welfare paid to single-mother families. That origin is still wired into the machinery. The clearest symptom is at the top: the federal office renamed itself from the Office of Child Support Enforcement to the Office of Child Support Services in June 2023, then back to Enforcement in December 2025. "Enforcement" evokes policing and shame; "services" signals help. An institution that cannot keep its own name has not decided whether its relationship to a parent is adversarial or supportive — and every downstream artifact (letter tone, portal language, enforcement triggers, caseworker scripts) inherits that unresolved question.

The program was engineered for a population that is now the minority of who it serves.

Built for 1975, serving 2026

The caseload has quietly inverted. The enforcement-first design assumes a welfare-linked family — but today only about 7% of cases are current-TANF and 38% formerly TANF; 55% have no welfare connection at all. Meanwhile, roughly half of custodial parents never pursue an order — the most common reasons being that they "did not feel the need to make it legal" or that "the other parent provides what he or she can." Families are opting out because informal arrangements already work for them, which means the system's biggest signal is often who it is losing.

And it is measurably losing them. IV-D caseloads fell 11.8% in five years — from 13.2M in FY2020 to 11.65M in FY2024 — even as single-parent families did not disappear. The most parsimonious read is that families with workable informal arrangements are increasingly declining a program whose dominant instrument is enforcement. For Deloitte GPS, that is the business case in a single statistic: a shrinking, distrustful caseload is a legitimacy problem that modernization exists to reverse. And the stakes for those who stay are high — when child support does arrive, it is roughly 42% of household income for the family receiving it.

The arrears trap

That $115.7B in arrears is not a backlog waiting to be collected — for many families it is a permanent disqualification from formal economic life. A large share is assigned (welfare-reimbursement) debt the family never sees and the state cannot forgive without a federal waiver. It compounds exactly where families can least absorb it: roughly 15% of imprisoned parents carry support orders, and ~78% of them keep accruing debt while incarcerated with no automatic pause, while interest keeps running (6% a year in Virginia). The scholarship calls this the "dual-debtor" problem — the tools meant to compel payment instead destroy the earning capacity that would have produced it.

The line you can move — and the line you cannot

A strategic distinction runs through the whole project: which pains are design problems your team can redesign, and which are policy problems set in statute. The order-amount formula (e.g., Georgia O.C.G.A. § 19-6-15), the enforcement tools (income withholding, tax-refund intercept, license suspension, passport denial, bank levy, liens, civil contempt), and the rule that government-owed arrears cannot be forgiven without a federal waiver are the fixed legal core. But letter tone, portal architecture, phone-tree design, caseworker tooling, notification channels, payment UX, and the explanation of how a number was reached are all design surfaces — and that is where nearly all the felt cost lives. The clearest proof that these are choices, not law: payment-card vendors and their fee schedules are procurement decisions, not statute — which is precisely why a case like Sparksman v. Comerica, challenging fee-laden disbursement debit cards, can even be litigated.

Threaded at the macro level — what's working / what's failing
Working
  • The federal locate infrastructure (FPLS, National Directory of New Hires, Federal Case Registry) is genuinely powerful — it can find a parent, employer, and income across state lines.
  • Income withholding carries 71% of all dollars reliably and invisibly, with no friction for the family on the happy path.
  • A 66% federal match gives states real capacity to deliver a service at scale.
Failing
  • Fragmentation: "it depends on the state" fractures every cross-state journey and makes the program illegible nationally.
  • The §458 incentives reward throughput and dollars, not fairness — so the funding formula structurally discourages "sitting with the person."
  • Cost-recovery retention diverts $807.8M of paid support away from families to government — the single most legitimacy-destroying hidden step.
  • Mandatory TANF cooperation can break functioning co-parenting arrangements, and the same locate tools (FPLS, NDNH) that find a non-paying parent can expose a survivor fleeing intimate-partner violence — a safety risk the system rarely accounts for.
  • The caseload is voting with its feet: an 11.8% five-year decline signals families opting out of an enforcement-dominant program faster than it can modernize.

Meso — the service

How the program actually operates: the case lifecycle, the way it differs from state to state, the blueprint of who does what, and the people in the room.

The case lifecycle — nine steps

Every case moves through some path across nine steps. Many cases skip steps or loop back (a closed-looking case returns to modify years later). The green tags map each step onto the six journey stages your project is organized around. Step 6 is set apart because it is where the program's human cost concentrates.

1

Intake / application / referral

A parent applies voluntarily (a one-time $25 fee) or a public-assistance case is auto-referred from TANF/Medicaid with no choice. The state agency opens the case and assigns a caseworker.

Actors: custodial parent · agency  |  Branch: voluntary vs. mandatory · paternity already established?
Stage · Case initiation
2

Locate

Find the other parent, their employer, income, and assets — often across state lines.

Systems: FPLS · National Directory of New Hires · Federal Case Registry
Stage · Locate
3

Establish paternity

Required for non-marital cases before an order can issue. Voluntary Acknowledgment at the birth hospital, or court-ordered genetic testing if contested.

Actors: parents · hospital · vital records · courts
Stage · Establishment
4

Establish the order

The state's guideline calculates the amount; a court (or the agency administratively) issues a formal order. Income is imputed if the non-custodial parent doesn't appear.

Branch: present vs. absent · income documented vs. imputed · medical support included?
Stage · Establishment
5

Collect & disburse

An Income Withholding Order goes to the employer → employer remits to the State Disbursement Unit → SDU pays the family. This is the invisible "happy path" for most dollars.

Actors: employer · SDU · banks  |  Branch: employed vs. self-employed/unbanked
Stage · Financials
6

Enforce — when payment lapses

The agency escalates: tax-refund intercept, credit reporting, driver's / professional / recreational license suspension, passport denial, bank levy, liens, and civil contempt (which can mean jail). This is the exception path — and where almost all felt pain lives.

Branch: which tool first? · notice given? · ability-to-pay screened? · is suspension counterproductive?
Stage · Enforcement
7

Modify

Review is mandatory every three years for TANF cases; otherwise either party must request it and prove a "substantial change in circumstances." Rarely proactive.

Branch: job loss / incarceration / illness? · who initiated? · reviewed proactively?
Stage · Case management
8

Distribute

Where the money goes. Non-TANF families get 100%; current/former-TANF collections may be retained to reimburse government. Pass-through and arrears rules vary sharply by state.

Branch: TANF status? · state pass-through policy? · arrears vs. current support
Stage · Financials
9

Close

The child reaches majority, arrears are satisfied, or other statutory criteria are met. The case can stay open for years if arrears remain.

Branch: outstanding arrears? · still a minor? · CP requested closure?
Stage · Case management

Why "it depends on the state" — three process archetypes

The nine-step skeleton is federally mandated, so it is the same everywhere. What varies sits on three axes: the guideline model (how the amount is calculated), the administration model (who runs it — state agency, county, or courts), and the distribution model (whether the family or the government gets the money first). Clustering states into three archetypes makes the variation legible without memorizing fifty-four programs.

A fourth model worth naming for your project: tribal programs (~60 nations) are genuinely distinct — smaller, community-embedded, operating under tribal courts and able to recognize non-cash and in-kind support that the formal state system cannot. They are the clearest example of a humane delivery model already operating inside the same federal frame.

One distribution reform stands out as a rare triple win: full pass-through — sending collected support to the family instead of retaining it to reimburse government — simultaneously raises payment, raises receipt, and signals the system is on the family's side. California, Illinois, Maryland, New Mexico, Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Wisconsin, and Wyoming have phased it in (Oregon is considering it), and San Francisco's in-kind child support pilot goes further still, testing recognition of the non-cash support the formal system normally ignores.

Threaded at the meso level — what's working / what's failing
Working
  • Income withholding is the single most effective collection method ever built — reliable, automatic, low-friction.
  • Voluntary paternity acknowledgment at the hospital is a quiet, well-designed success.
  • Evidence-backed bright spots exist: procedural-justice casework (PJAC) and employment-first models (CSPED) measurably raise fairness and satisfaction; portal modernization (Idaho Nexi, Conduent THRIVE) cuts caseworker load.
Failing
  • Legacy mainframes — some 30+ years old — force caseworkers to re-key the same data across systems verify.
  • Orders are often set above ability to pay; in ~55% of low-income CSPED cases the order exceeded half of earnings.
  • Modification is reactive: orders aren't reviewed automatically when a parent loses a job or is incarcerated, so debt compounds.
  • Wide judicial discretion produces wildly inconsistent outcomes — the same facts yield jail in one courtroom and patience in another.
Level 03zoom: ground level · the person

Micro — the person

What it feels like from inside the system. The emotional curve from the blueprint, opened up into lived experience — and the one thing that reliably earns trust.

The four questions a parent is really asking

Procedural-justice research (notably MDRC's PJAC demonstration) shows that people cooperate with an outcome they dislike if the process felt fair — and fairness reduces to four questions. They double as a design rubric: score every touchpoint against them.

Voice — "Was I heard?"
Parents want to explain their situation before a decision, not after enforcement starts. Orders are still set in their absence through imputed-income proceedings.
Neutrality — "Was it even-handed?"
Consistent rules applied the same way to everyone — undercut by judicial discretion and a structure that points the enforcement apparatus at only one parent.
Respect — "Was I treated with dignity?"
The single most-cited non-custodial theme: being treated "like a criminal" rather than a parent trying to provide.
Trust — "Did I understand it?"
Opacity reads as arbitrariness. A parent who can't see how a number was reached can't experience it as fair, even when it's legally correct.
I'm talking to Molly instead of talking to child support.Non-custodial parent · MDRC PJAC parent reflections

That sentence is the whole finding. Trust attaches to a named, reachable human — "Molly" — not to an institution. In live agency reviews, the rare five-star ratings almost always credit one specific caseworker by name. The corresponding failure is just as concrete: "the people who answer the phone are not necessarily putting you in touch with that person."

How each stakeholder feels — and why

Custodial parentpriority 1 · the client
Exhausted, distrustful of promises, financially precarious — hope and disappointment cycling with each missed payment.Why: sporadic/partial payments, opaque case status, re-traumatizing intake, and the sense of being an afterthought in a process nominally about her own child.
Non-custodial parentpriority 2 · the target
Alienated, ashamed, fearful of jail or losing a license — bitterness at a system read as structurally against him. The defining reframe: dead broke, not deadbeat — most nonpayment is inability, not refusal (65% previously incarcerated; ~1/3 earning too little to support themselves).Why: orders above ability to pay, a debt spiral that survives even incarceration, no link between paying and seeing the child, and enforcement letters that drive withdrawal rather than payment (the "turtle effect").
Caseworkerpriority 3 · front line
Overwhelmed, morally strained — often genuine care colliding with an enforcement mandate.Why: large, randomly-assigned caseloads on aging tools, pushed toward "process the case" by metrics that count throughput, not whether a parent felt heard.
The childpriority 4 · absent
No recorded voice at all.Why: the stated beneficiary of the entire system, yet no data is collected from the child's perspective. Naming this absence is often the sharpest insight in a child-support redesign.

The dimension that runs through all four

Race cannot be treated as a subtheme. Black non-custodial parents are dramatically overrepresented in contempt and incarceration outcomes; in the CSPED sample, 40% of non-custodial parents were Black and 65% had been incarcerated; in Illinois, Black families were 111% more likely than White families to be sanctioned (2018–19). The mechanism is wide judicial discretion — the same facts producing radically different outcomes depending on the judge. Agency staffing has historically not reflected the communities served, compounding the gap on every procedural-justice dimension at once.

Threaded at the micro level — what's working / what's failing
Working
  • One thing reliably earns trust: a named, reachable caseworker who explains a decision before it's issued. PJAC case managers who gave out direct numbers let parents bypass the phone queue entirely.
  • When human attention rises, so does perceived fairness — CSPED's extra-services group reported 68% "treated fairly" vs. 53%, on ~3.7× the contact hours.
  • Removing the most punitive step barely dents collections: PJAC cut civil-contempt filings ~60 points with negligible payment loss.
Failing
  • Opacity-as-arbitrariness: parents can't decode how the order or the arrears were calculated, so even correct numbers feel unjust.
  • No dedicated, reachable caseworker for most parents; status is invisible; court dates and enforcement actions arrive without notice.
  • Shame and fear actively suppress payment — the system's tone works against its own goal.

Level 05downstream · not the focus yet

Opportunity space

Kept deliberately light. These are the highest-legitimacy, lowest-cost design levers the evidence already supports — flagged for later, not the current phase.

lever · respect + trust
A named, reachable caseworker
A "who is my caseworker" field plus a direct line. The single most-cited difference between a one-star and a five-star experience.
lever · trust
"How we got this number"
A plain-language explainer attached to every order and enforcement action, turning opacity into something a parent can see and accept.
lever · voice
Proactive status notifications
Channel-chosen SMS/email for court dates, payments, and enforcement — so no one learns of a court date after it happened.
lever · access
Fee-free, mobile-first payment
Fast-posting digital payment for unbanked parents — a pure-design fix to a pain point that feels structural.

All four sit on the design side of the design-vs-policy line drawn at the macro level — they require no statutory change, only a decision to build them.
